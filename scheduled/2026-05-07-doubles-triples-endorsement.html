CDL Doubles and Triples Endorsement: Complete Guide (2026)

Updated: May 2026Read time: 8 min

The Doubles and Triples endorsement (coded "T" on your CDL) is required to pull two or three trailers at once. It's one of the easiest endorsements to get — just a written test — and it opens up a specific set of well-paying routes that many drivers overlook.

📋 Doubles & Triples Fast Facts

Required for: Pulling 2 or 3 trailers simultaneously. Getting it: 1 written test at DMV. States allowing triple trailers: About 20 states. Pay premium over single trailer: $3,000–$10,000/year. Time to add: 1 DMV visit.

What the Doubles and Triples Endorsement Covers

The T endorsement allows you to operate:

  • B-train doubles — Two 28-foot trailers connected directly (common in LTL freight)
  • Rocky Mountain doubles — One 48-foot trailer plus one 28-foot trailer
  • Triple trailers — Three 28-foot trailers (only legal in certain states)
  • Turnpike doubles — Two full 53-foot trailers (limited to specific toll roads)

How to Get the Doubles and Triples Endorsement

  1. Study the doubles and triples section of your state CDL manual — covers coupling and uncoupling procedures, handling characteristics, and safety rules specific to multi-trailer operations.
  2. Pass the written test at your state DMV — typically 20 questions. Pass rate is high with minimal study.
  3. Pay the state fee — usually $10–$30.

No additional driving test or background check is required. Same-day processing at most DMV offices.

Which States Allow Triple Trailers?

Triple trailers are not legal on all roads in all states. States with permitted triple operations include: Arizona, Colorado, Idaho, Indiana, Kansas, Montana, Nebraska, Nevada, North Dakota, Oklahoma, Oregon, South Dakota, Utah, Washington, and Wyoming — primarily western states with wide interstates and lower traffic density. Eastern states generally restrict vehicles to doubles only.

Who Hires Doubles and Triples Drivers

  • LTL carriers — Old Dominion, XPO, Estes Express, and other LTL companies run doubles extensively for their linehaul operations
  • USPS and FedEx — Run doubles for mail and package distribution between major hubs
  • UPS — Extensive doubles operation especially for overnight linehaul
  • Regional LTL companies — Many regional carriers use doubles for trunk line routes

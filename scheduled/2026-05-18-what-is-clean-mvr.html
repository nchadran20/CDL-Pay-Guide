What Is a Clean MVR and Why Does It Matter for CDL Drivers? (2026 Guide)

Updated: May 2026Read time: 8 min

Your MVR — Motor Vehicle Record — is your driving history. For CDL drivers, it's one of the most important documents in your career. Carriers pull it before every hire, insurance companies use it to set rates, and violations on it can cost you thousands of dollars in lost wages or missed job opportunities. Here's what you need to know.

📋 MVR Fast Facts

What it is: Your complete driving history from your state DMV. Who checks it: Every carrier before hiring, plus your current employer annually. Lookback period: Most carriers check 3–10 years. Most damaging violations: DUI, reckless driving, serious speeding, leaving the scene.

What an MVR Contains

Your Motor Vehicle Record is a report from your state's DMV that shows:

  • All traffic violations and convictions
  • Accidents you were involved in
  • License suspensions and revocations
  • DUI/DWI convictions
  • License class and endorsements
  • Points on your license (in states that use a point system)
  • License reinstatement records

What "Clean MVR" Means in Practice

Carriers define "clean MVR" differently, but a generally accepted standard is:

  • No DUI/DWI convictions in the past 5–10 years
  • No reckless driving convictions in the past 3–5 years
  • No more than 2–3 minor moving violations in the past 3 years
  • No serious speeding violations (15+ mph over the limit) in the past 3 years
  • No at-fault accidents in the past 3 years
  • No license suspensions in the past 3–5 years

The CSA Score — Beyond Your MVR

The FMCSA also maintains a Compliance, Safety, Accountability (CSA) score for CDL drivers based on roadside inspection results. Your CSA score is separate from your MVR but carriers can see it. Violations that appear in roadside inspections go into your PSP (Pre-Employment Screening Program) record — another database carriers pull when hiring. A clean MVR with a bad PSP/CSA record can still cost you job opportunities.

How to Protect Your MVR

  • Drive consistently within the law — This sounds obvious but CDL drivers are held to a higher standard than regular drivers. What's a minor ticket for a regular driver can be a "serious violation" for a CDL holder.
  • Contest tickets when appropriate — A single serious violation can cost you significantly more in lost wages and hiring opportunities than the cost of fighting the ticket in court. Many CDL attorneys specialize in traffic violations for commercial drivers.
  • Know the CDL standard — You can be cited for serious violations in your personal vehicle. CDL holders are governed by higher standards even off-duty.
  • Check your own MVR annually — Request a copy from your state DMV to verify accuracy. Errors do appear and you want to catch and correct them before a carrier does.

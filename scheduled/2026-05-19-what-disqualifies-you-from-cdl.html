What Disqualifies You from Getting a CDL? (2026 Complete List)

Updated: May 2026Read time: 8 min

Before investing time and money into CDL school, it's worth knowing whether anything in your history could prevent you from getting or keeping a CDL. Here's the complete list of disqualifying factors — medical, criminal, and driving — organized clearly so you can assess your eligibility before you start.

📋 The Good News

Most people who ask this question qualify for a CDL. The disqualifying factors are specific and well-defined. If you have a clean driving record and no major criminal history, you almost certainly qualify. Read on to confirm.

Medical Disqualifications

The DOT physical determines whether you're medically qualified to drive commercially. Disqualifying conditions include:

  • Epilepsy or seizure disorders — Any seizure within the past 10 years is disqualifying unless you have a seizure-free period with physician documentation and FMCSA waiver
  • Vision below 20/40 in either eye (uncorrectable) — Correctable to 20/40 with glasses or contacts is permitted
  • Loss of a limb or hand impairment — May qualify with a Skills Performance Evaluation (SPE) certificate from FMCSA
  • Insulin-dependent diabetes — Requires participation in the FMCSA's insulin-treated diabetes mellitus (ITDM) program
  • Severe cardiovascular conditions — Recent heart attack, angina, coronary artery disease requiring treatment may disqualify
  • Current drug or alcohol dependency — Active addiction is disqualifying; recovery with documentation may allow reinstatement
  • Uncontrolled blood pressure above 180/110 — Must be controlled with treatment to qualify
  • Hearing impairment — Cannot perceive a forced whisper at 5 feet with better ear (with hearing aid if used)

Driving Record Disqualifications

Certain patterns of driving violations can result in CDL suspension or disqualification:

  • Two serious violations in 3 years (speeding 15+ mph over, reckless driving, following too close, improper lane change, tailgating) — 60-day CDL suspension
    • Three serious violations in 3 years — 120-day CDL suspension
  • Two railroad crossing violations in 3 years — 60-day suspension
  • Three railroad crossing violations in 3 years — 1-year disqualification
  • Out-of-service order violation (driving while ordered out of service) — 90 to 180 days for first offense

Things That Do NOT Disqualify You

Many people worry unnecessarily about things that do not actually prevent CDL eligibility:

  • No college degree required — CDL is entirely skills and test based
  • Age 18 is old enough — For intrastate driving; 21 for interstate
  • Glasses or contacts permitted — As long as vision corrects to 20/40
  • Single past DUI (10+ years ago) — Generally no longer a barrier at most carriers
  • Most non-violent felonies — Not automatically disqualifying by federal law (though carrier policies vary)
  • Bankruptcy — No bearing on CDL eligibility
  • Hearing aid users — Permitted if you can perceive a whisper at 5 feet with the aid

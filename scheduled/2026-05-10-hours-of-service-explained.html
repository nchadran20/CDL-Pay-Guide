What Is Hours of Service (HOS)? Simple Explanation for CDL Drivers (2026)

Updated: May 2026Read time: 8 min

Hours of Service rules are federal regulations that limit how long CDL drivers can drive before they must rest. They exist to prevent fatigued driving — and violating them carries serious penalties. Here's a plain-English explanation of every HOS rule you need to know in 2026.

⚠️ Why This Matters

HOS violations carry fines of up to $16,000 per violation and can result in your vehicle being put out of service roadside. Understanding HOS is not optional — it directly controls how many miles you can run and therefore how much you earn.

The 14-Hour Rule — The One Drivers Misunderstand Most

This is the rule that catches new drivers off guard. The 14-hour window starts the moment you come on duty — not when you start driving. If you spend 3 hours at a loading dock before you drive a single mile, those 3 hours count against your 14-hour window. You now have only 11 hours left in your window, even though you haven't started your 11 driving hours yet.

This is why experienced drivers manage loading and unloading times carefully and why detention time (waiting at a shipper or receiver) is such a significant issue for driver earnings.

The 30-Minute Break Rule

After 8 cumulative hours of driving (not on-duty time — specifically driving time), you must take at least a 30-minute break. The break must be off-duty or sleeper berth time. You can split it across two stops of 15+ minutes each. Smart drivers time this break to coincide with fuel stops or mandatory wait times so it doesn't cut into productive driving time.

Sleeper Berth Provisions

If your truck has a sleeper berth, special rules apply that allow you to split your 10-hour off-duty period:

  • You can split the required 10 hours off as long as one period is at least 7 hours in the sleeper berth and the other is at least 2 hours (either in the sleeper or off duty)
  • Neither period counts against your 14-hour window
  • The two periods combined must total at least 10 hours

Short-Haul Exception — For Local Drivers

Drivers who operate within 150 air miles of their reporting location and return to their starting point within 14 hours may qualify for the short-haul exception, which exempts them from certain logging requirements. Local delivery drivers should ask their carrier if they qualify — it significantly reduces administrative burden.

ELDs — Electronic Logging Devices

Since 2017, most commercial drivers are required to use an Electronic Logging Device (ELD) that automatically tracks driving time and enforces HOS rules. The ELD connects to the truck's engine and records when the vehicle moves. You cannot manually override it. HOS is no longer a matter of paperwork — ELDs enforce it automatically and roadside inspectors can pull the data instantly.

How HOS Affects Your Earnings

Understanding HOS lets you maximize legal miles. Strategies experienced drivers use:

  • Start driving immediately after your 10-hour break rather than spending time on non-driving tasks first
  • Choose drop-and-hook freight to avoid dock time eating into your 14-hour window
  • Time your 30-minute break during mandatory waits rather than stopping on the road
  • Use the 34-hour restart strategically to reset your weekly hours when running hard

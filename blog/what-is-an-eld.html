What Is an ELD? Electronic Logging Device Explained for Truck Drivers (2026)

Updated: May 2026Read time: 8 min

An ELD — Electronic Logging Device — is a piece of technology mounted in your truck that automatically records your driving time and enforces Hours of Service rules. Since 2019, ELDs have been required for the vast majority of commercial drivers. Here's everything you need to know about how they work and how they affect your daily life as a driver.

📱 ELD Fast Facts

Required for: Most CDL drivers operating commercial motor vehicles. What it does: Automatically tracks driving time, enforces HOS rules, and creates an electronic log. Cost: Paid by carrier — drivers don't purchase their own. Exemptions: Short-haul drivers, drivers of pre-2000 model year trucks, some ag operations.

What an ELD Actually Does

An ELD is a device — usually a tablet or dedicated screen mounted in the cab — that connects directly to your truck's engine control module (ECM). It automatically detects when the vehicle is moving and records it as driving time in your electronic log. Key functions:

  • Automatic duty status recording — When the truck moves, it records as driving. You manually set off-duty, on-duty/not driving, and sleeper berth status when appropriate.
  • HOS enforcement — Tracks your 11-hour, 14-hour, and weekly limits in real time. Alerts you before you approach a violation.
  • Log creation — Creates a digital record of every duty status change, accessible to DOT inspectors and your carrier.
  • Inspection transfer — Can send your logs to a roadside inspector wirelessly or via display — no paper logs needed for most situations.
  • Location tracking — Records GPS position throughout your duty period.

How an ELD Affects Your Daily Driving

As a driver, the ELD primarily means:

  • Your logs are automatic — less paperwork and less chance of logging errors
  • Your HOS is enforced in real time — the ELD will warn you as you approach limits
  • Your carrier can see your location and status at all times
  • Roadside inspections are faster — inspectors pull your logs electronically in seconds
  • You cannot fudge your logs — the ELD records actual engine data that cannot be altered

Who Is Exempt from the ELD Mandate

  • Drivers using the short-haul exception who operate within 150 air miles of their home terminal
  • Drivers of commercial motor vehicles manufactured before model year 2000
  • Drivers who use paper logs no more than 8 days in any 30-day period
  • Drivers transporting livestock or bees (special agricultural exemptions)
  • Driveaway-towaway operations where the vehicle is the commodity

ELDs and Owner-Operators

If you're an owner-operator, you're responsible for having a compliant ELD in your truck. You cannot use a non-registered device — the FMCSA maintains a list of registered ELD providers. Non-compliant ELDs result in violations during inspections. The cost is typically $20–$40/month as a subscription for a registered device.

How Do Trucking Companies Pay Drivers? Per Mile vs Hourly vs Salary (2026)

Updated: May 2026Read time: 8 min

One of the most confusing things about trucking for new drivers is how pay actually works. Unlike most jobs with an hourly wage, many trucking positions pay by the mile — which means your earnings depend entirely on how many miles you run. Here's exactly how each pay structure works, which pays the most, and how to calculate what you'll really earn.

💰 The Three Pay Structures

Per mile: Most common for OTR and regional. Pay depends on miles driven. Hourly: Common for local delivery. Predictable but typically lower annual earnings. Salary: Less common — usually for private fleets or specialized positions. Guaranteed income regardless of miles.

Per Mile Pay — The Most Common Structure

Per mile (also called "cents per mile" or CPM) is the dominant pay structure for OTR and most regional driving. You earn a set rate for every mile the loaded truck moves.

What Counts as a "Mile" in Per-Mile Pay?

This matters more than most new drivers realize. Most carriers pay on practical miles — a routing software calculation of the most practical route between pickup and delivery. This is usually shorter than the actual GPS miles you drive. Some carriers pay on household goods miles (HHG miles) which are even shorter. Ask your carrier exactly which mileage system they use before accepting a job offer.

Empty Miles and Deadhead

When you drive without a load (deadhead or bobtail) to pick up your next load, many carriers do not pay full CPM — or pay nothing at all for those miles. Some carriers pay 50–75% of their loaded rate for empty miles. This is a significant factor in your actual earnings — ask about deadhead pay policy when evaluating carriers.

Accessorial Pay — The Hidden Income

Smart drivers pay attention to accessorial pay — extra compensation on top of the base per-mile rate:

  • Stop pay: $15–$50 per additional stop beyond the first
  • Detention pay: $15–$25/hour after 2 hours waiting at a shipper or receiver
  • Layover pay: $75–$200/day when forced to wait due to shipper delays
  • Tarp pay: $25–$75 for tarping flatbed loads
  • Hazmat pay: $0.02–$0.05/mile premium for hauling hazardous materials
  • Team pay: Higher CPM for two-driver teams running nearly continuously

Hourly Pay — Best for Local Driving

Local delivery drivers are typically paid hourly. This provides predictable income regardless of traffic, detention, or loading delays — but usually results in lower annual earnings than per-mile OTR driving.

Salary Pay — Private Fleets and Specialized Roles

Some positions — particularly private fleet driving for large companies (Walmart, HEB, Costco, Amazon) — offer a guaranteed annual salary regardless of miles. These positions are highly sought after because:

  • Income is predictable and guaranteed
  • Benefits are often superior to commercial carriers
  • Routes are typically consistent and familiar
  • Home time is usually better than OTR

The tradeoff is that these jobs are harder to get — usually requiring 1–3 years of prior experience and a spotless driving record. Private fleet jobs typically pay $65,000–$90,000/year in salary.

Which Pay Structure Earns the Most?

Over a full career, experienced OTR per-mile drivers who run high miles consistently earn the most in raw dollars. But the lifestyle trade-off is significant — you're away from home constantly. Local hourly jobs and private fleet salary positions often provide the best combination of income and quality of life for drivers with 2+ years of experience.

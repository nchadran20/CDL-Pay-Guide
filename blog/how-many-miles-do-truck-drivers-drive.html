How Many Miles Do Truck Drivers Drive Per Day? (2026 Real Numbers)

Updated: May 2026Read time: 8 min

Miles per day is one of the most important numbers in a truck driver's earnings — because most OTR and regional drivers are paid per mile, not per hour. The more miles you run, the more you earn. Here's what the real numbers look like across different driving types in 2026.

📊 Average Daily Miles by Driving Type

OTR driver: 500–700 miles/day. Regional driver: 400–550 miles/day. Local driver: 100–250 miles/day. Team driving (combined): 900–1,200 miles/day. These are real-world averages accounting for loading, unloading, inspections, and HOS restrictions.

Federal Hours of Service Rules — The Daily Mile Limit

Federal Hours of Service (HOS) regulations cap how much a CDL driver can drive in a single day. These are not suggestions — they are federal law enforced through Electronic Logging Devices (ELDs):

  • 11-hour driving limit — You may drive a maximum of 11 hours after 10 consecutive hours off duty
  • 14-hour window — You cannot drive beyond the 14th hour after coming on duty, regardless of breaks taken
  • 30-minute break requirement — Must take a 30-minute break after 8 cumulative hours of driving
  • 60/70-hour weekly limit — Cannot drive after accumulating 60 hours in 7 days or 70 hours in 8 days

At 65 mph average (factoring in traffic, stops, and loading), 11 hours of driving produces approximately 650–715 miles. In practice, most drivers average 500–650 miles per day due to traffic, loading delays, and mandatory breaks.

What Reduces Your Daily Miles

  • Traffic — Urban routes, LA, NYC, Chicago all cut into miles significantly
  • Loading and unloading time — Waiting at docks can eat 2–4 hours of your 14-hour window
  • Weather — Ice, snow, and fog slow you down or force stops
  • Detention time — Shippers who make you wait reduce your available driving hours (many carriers pay detention after 2 hours)
  • Inexperience — New drivers take longer to set up routes, navigate terminals, and manage HOS efficiently

How to Maximize Your Daily Miles

  • Plan your 14-hour window carefully — know when it starts and ends
  • Use your 30-minute break strategically during fuel stops or mandatory waits
  • Choose drop-and-hook freight over live loading when possible — eliminates dock wait time
  • Drive during off-peak hours (overnight, early morning) to avoid traffic
  • Know your truck's speed settings — a governor at 65 vs 70 mph makes a significant difference annually
